Research

My interests focus on the applications of data science and digitisation to practical inorganic chemistry. Key themes include: the discovery of novel species; devising strategies for efficient exploration; and developing new digital tools for chemists.

Developing New Chemical Exploration Strategies

There are many ways to guide chemical discovery, including: data from computational calculations or previous literature, human intuition/heuristics, or the empirical results of an ongoing exploration within a particular search space.

Chemical discovery often relies on serendipity, and one of my key interests is in the development of novel quasi-random screening methods to enable this. In addition, human/robot teams have been shown to outperform either entity alone in the crystallisation of giant polyoxometalates (Link), and I am interested in finding robust methods to allow human intuition to inform automated exploration, without harming the integrity of the exploration or removing the opportunity for serendipity.


Discovery Chemistry for Complex Systems

Discovery chemistry usually operates on a molecular scale. Developing techniques to permit discovery using data collected on a complex chemical system removes the necessity to invest time in the purification of individual species prior to assessing the system and allows for molecular interactions to be taken into account when screening for function.

Mass spectrometry data is unparalleled in the deconvolution of complex systems, where the concentration-dependence of many other analytical techniques makes them unsuitable. Automated interpretation and quantification of large MS datasets (without prior knowledge of products) allows the identification of product distributions and may then permit their isolation. Through this, we have proposed a library generation method and data analysis workflow to combine with a functional assay and enable screening for lead systems.
